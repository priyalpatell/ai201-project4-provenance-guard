# Provenance Guard

This system will analyze creative text content to classify as AI text, human text, or unable to distinguish. It will score confidence and provide labels to users.

# Transparency Label Design

These examples will be provided to LLM based generate_label(), which will create a one-sentence label from the reasoning provided in the LLM-based classification, llm_score, and sylometric_score.
attribution: `likely AI-generated`
label: We found highly predictable phrasing alongside a lack of variation in sentence length and vocabulary, which strongly matches AI-generated writing.

attribution: `likely human-written`
label: We found natural contextual phrasing and dynamic variation in both sentence length and vocabulary, which cleanly matches human writing.

attribution: `uncertain`
label: We detected a mix of natural word choices alongside rigid, uniform sentence structures. Because the layout lacks clear certainty, please submit an appeal for review.

# Multi-signal Detection Pipeline

Signal 1: `LLM-based classification`
Function: get_LLM_score()
Measures: Captures the semantic meaning the context, tone, and how ideas flow. Human writing is often more neuanced and unpredictable involving more diverse language use and informal transitions. While AI writing, it more predictable and formulaic with typically more use of structured transition phrases.
Limitations: It can be blinded to text that sounds human like, while missing the uniform structure of the text.
Usage: Analyzes the text input given and outputs a confidence score between 0.0 (human-written) and 1.0 (AI-generated) with a brief 1-2 sentence explanation. The output will formated as a JSON with reasoning appearing first and then llm_score.

Signal 2: `Stylometric heuristics`
Function: get_stylometric_score()
Measures: Captures the statistical patterns such as the sentence length variation or use of unqiue words in the type-token ratio. Typically human writing will use more sentences of various lengths and will be more verbous. While AI writing, will use more uniform sentence length and less diverse vocabulary.
Limitations: It does not focus on the actual words utilized in the text so can miss key contextual clues. These metrics could be inconsistent if either type of writing differs from typical structure.
Usage: Calculates the probability score between 0.0 (human-written) and 1.0 (AI-generated) by averaging the two stylometric statistics. It first finds sentence length variation by averaging the squared differences of each sentence from the mean length, normalizing and limiting to 100, and inverting by 1.0-score so uniform sentence lengths yield a higher AI probability. Finds vocabulary diversity by dividing unique words by total word count and inverts 1.0-score so more common phrasing is attributed to higher AI probability.

# Confidence Score with Uncertainty

This scoring setup is designed to trust the author and avoid falsely accusing a human writer. The final score is calculated by giving a 60% weight to the LLM semantic score and a 40% weight to the stylometric score; since AI could micmic human stylometrics or humans could also write more uniformly this is why more emphasis is placed on semantic meaning of the text. This combined score is then used to sort the text into clear categories based on the level of certainty.

total confidence score = (0.6 _ LLM score) + (0.4 _ stylometric score)

```
0.0 -------------- 0.50 ----------------- 0.80 --------- 1.0
|   likely-human     |      uncertain       |   likely-AI   |
```

# Edge cases

Case 1: Academic pappers or legal documents use a formal tone and could naturally have more uniform sentence lengths, making it harder to distinguish this as human-written and not AI-generated.
Case 2: Tweets, reviews, or smaller pieces of writing could lack variety in vocabulary leading to lower TTR score, which could make it difficult to flag this as human-written.
Case 3: Writing generated by AI that is explicitly tailored to micmic human variance in sentence length and vocabulary could lead to lower stylometric score and possibly lead to uncertain or incorrect human-written attribution.

# Audit Log

The log will capture every attribution decision and appeals. The entries will have these fields: content_id, creator_id, timestamp, attribution, confidence, llm_score, stylometric_score, status, and optional creator_reasoning. I will use sqlite to store these entries and datetime to calculate the timestamp.

# Appeals Workflow

When a user submits an appeal, the content-id will be used to find the item in the SQLite table and update the status to "under review". Also the optional field "creator_reasoning" will be updated with the input reasoning collected. A final message will be return to the user that the appeal was received and is under review.

# API Endpoints

The flask app will have the follow endpoints:
`POST /submit`
Will collect the text and creator_id provided by the user. It will then call the multi-signal pipeline first the LLM classification, get_LLM_score() and then the stylometric heuristics, get_stylometric_score(). It will use these scores to calculate the final confidence score outline in the "Confidence Score with Uncertainty" section.

If the normalized llm_score and stylometric_score are both within the 0.0-1.0 range, it will pass llm_score, reasoning provided by the LLM, and stylometric score to generate_label().
It will log all data points as specified in the "Audit Log" section. It will jsonify and return the content_id, confidence, attribution, and label.

It will fail gracefully if only one score is correctly provided within the 0.0-1.0 range then it will set the confidence to this score and determine the attribution. This singular score will be passed to generate_label(). The score that does not get provided will be logged as NaN value. It will return jsonify of the content_id, confidence, attribution, and label.

`POST /appeal`
Will collect the content_id and creator_reasoning provided by the user. It will then call update_event() function which find the entry by the content_id and will create entry where the entry's status to "under review", set the optional "creator_reasoning" field to the one provided. It will return jsonify of the content_id, status, and message of "Your appeal was received and is under review.".

`GET /log`
Will return jsonify of entries which is set to the result of read_log().

# Rate limiting

Usually content creation takes time even in the short form I feel a limit of 20 submissions per day is generous and sufficient to handle both short and long text content. Similarly, 20 appeals one per submission is appropriate. An adversary would try to bombard the system and prevent users from accessing it by spamming submissions or appeals.

# Architecture

The submission flow processes user text through two distinct signal to calculate a combined confidence score and attribution, which then generates a transparency label and appends a row to the audit log database. The appeal flow allows creators to contest their classification by taking their content ID and reasoning, updating the entry's status, and appending this as a new entry to the audit log. Both flows strictly append new entries to the SQLite database rather than overwriting existing records to maintain a clear audit log.

